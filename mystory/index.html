A wise and influential filmmaker once cautioned me, "To fall in love with filmmaking is life's greatest curse."

At my own peril, I ignored my uncle's advice.

MY LIFE STORY.

The Search for Meaning.

I TOOK IN the numbers. The formulas. The equations. This was the last requisite class to graduate as an engineer.

Only that I wasn’t ready to graduate. And I wasn’t really an engineer. I was a fraud. An artist trapped in an engineer’s body.


A fish out of water.

A misfit.

S H A R E        

6 years earlier, I began creating my own liberal arts education in Cooper Union, a tuition-free university in New York that offered no such degree.

My pragmatic relatives in Singapore were wondering, “What’s he studying now? When’s he getting a job?”


Clearly, my idea of a university education was one that didn’t prepare me for the real world.

Ironically, that was why it was meaningful.

During my final year, I discovered film; the truly interdisciplinary medium I had been seeking to express myself as a visual artist.

That year I knew I had to be a filmmaker. That same year, I found out I had to give up being a filmmaker.

I finally graduated because I had to pull my weight in the family’s languishing food business.

Being a traveling salesman in the US meant surviving out of a suitcase, rental car and cheap motels.

No salary. No social life. No way out of the dreadful scripted cold calls every morning.


Is this what it’s like to have a job?

AFTER 2 YEARS, I returned to Singapore. I was looking forward to being waiter by day, screenwriter by night.

As if writing didn’t pay the bills, waitering in Singapore was worse.

An old friend I used to counsel sat me down. Quite awkwardly, it was his turn to ask,


"Is this how you plan to lead your life?"

One bar I worked at was a bankers' haunt. Through some connections, I worked my way to Morgan Stanley. Then investment banking and consulting in Hong Kong.

4 jobs in 2 years. At every job, I felt grossly overpaid because I produced subpar work.

I hated corporate life. I could hear my relatives go,


“Why can’t he hold on to a job?”

Was I unemployable?

I felt lonely.

Turns out several of my colleagues wanted to be entrepreneurs someday. They simply never acted on their dreams. And I echoed their sentiments.

My grand plan was to return to filmmaking by the age of 50.

My colleagues concurred. I thought to myself, "Really?"


Was I supposed to wait 20 years before embarking on my dreams?

Is this the Meaning of Life?

WITH ENOUGH SAVINGS to last a year, I quit the corporate world.

Moved to Tokyo. Lived out of a capsule hostel. At $10 a day, my space was slightly larger than a coffin.

I started trading currencies. It went nowhere. But it felt way better than hanging onto a job.

An old college friend had some ideas about trading the power transmission market. I had to re-wire my brain. Relearn coding and statistics. It was painful. But worth it.

By the end of the year, we were up and running. We traded from anywhere around the world.


For once, I felt truly free.

But absolute freedom guarantees absolute loneliness.

One time I transited in Hong Kong, I was introduced to a fashion designer who wanted to start his label in Europe.

So off we went. Berlin. Then Paris.

I helped him with finance. His designs were so esoteric. He never broke even.


I admired his passion.

His courage to pursue his dreams.

And wondered what happened to mine?

SO INSTEAD OF waiting 20 years to return to filmmaking, I went to London to study writing & directing.

I made films over the next few years. Yet within a year of making each and every one of them, I wanted to disown my babies.

Was I wrong about my passion for filmmaking?


This was supposed to be the meaningful life.

Or has it been a meaningless lie?

I was married by this time and I realized that my films didn’t have very much to say. I was merely in the business of filmmaking. Nothing more.


Why weren’t my films meaningful?

I longed to create work that I could get behind. Don't we all?

Film is an artistic medium with the potential of delivering a powerful message; a point of view that an artist truly cares about.


What did I care about?

A few years went by. My marriage broke down. I moved out. Resumed my nomadic life.

During my travels, I learned about a hermit warrior who had undergone a crazy transformation after the killing of his brother. He traveled the world to learn true self-defence. He became a teacher to protect and empower women.

I met Chye. Skipped the small talk. Got him to open up about the tragic life story that fuelled his social cause.


Not just the war stories that we love bragging about,

but the repressed ones we've waited our whole lives to get out.

ONE BY ONE, my subjects opened up to me about their life stories.

True stories are unmistakably heartfelt, messy, counterintuitive, and out of left field.

You get there by inhabiting the minds of your subjects. For me, it evolved out of a necessity to combat loneliness.

Why do we find true stories meaningful? Perhaps it's helpful to ask...


Do we actually seek the truth?

Or do we seek meaning?

Through these stories, I found my way home. My calling as a filmmaker who transcends visuals and self-expression.

When I’m asked today, "What is your job?" I say,


I’m a filmmaker.

My job is to move and inspire all of us...

To Stand for a Higher Cause.
